2016

Things Read

Throne of Glass: Sarah J. Maas; Fantasy
Terribly written first half and characters become better by the end, but I was told to get through this one so that I could move to the books afterwards which were apparently much improved. Overall a series of books about the chosen one and a lot of interesting world-building to demonstrate how interconnected everything is.
Crown of Midnight: Sarah J. Maas; Fantasy
The writing did improve. Lots of fleshing out of all the characters.
Heir of Fire: Sarah J. Maas; Fantasy
Queen of Shadows: Sarah J. Maas; Fantasy
Morning Star: Pierce Brown; SF
The conclusion to the Red Rising trilogy - I pre-ordered this immediately after finishing Golden Son and I don't have any regrets. Plenty of gut-wrenching twists.
The Effective Executive: The Definitive Guide to Getting the Right Things Done: Peter F. Drucker; Business
Full of a lot of decent advice about making sure that you are effective, and aimed towards the knowledge worker who is an executive whether they're the CEO or at the very bottom of the totem pole. Lots of good insights but a pretty dated writing style (possibly to do with the first publishing being in 1967), which made some points hit home a little less than they could have.
The Forever War: Joe Haldeman; SF
The first time I read this book it was immediately after reading Starship Troopers, and I missed the allegory entirely. This reading was much better; still a fantastic SF book.
Spelunky: Derek Yu; Games
Derek Yu tells the story of Spelunky, as well as reflecting a little bit on what brought him there. A really inspiring read.
Food Rules: An Eater's Manual: Michael Pollan
Michael Pollan distills all of the food and nutrition research he's read into simple, easy-to-follow rules about what you should eat. Takes about a half hour to read and you're guaranteed to come out with some simple heuristics that get you eating more of what you should.
The Creative Habit: Learn It and Use It for Life: Twyla Tharp
Twyla Tharp (a prolific choreographer) talks a bit about the preparation and effort that goes into her being as creative as possible at all times. A little weird at times, but a pretty good read.
Early Retirement Extreme: A philosophical and practical guide to financial independence: Jacob Lund Fisker; Personal Finance
I try to read one or two personal finance books a year; after seeing this one on Peter's list I figured I should check it out. It didn't grab me as much as I expected it to.

Games Played

Machi Koro
A really neat city building game, where you need to carefully manage your economy in order to build your city faster than anyone else. I enjoyed this a lot.
Flash Duel
Bought on Peter's recommendation; basically Street Fighter as Board Game. I'm impressed by the depth and really liked it, although I haven't had a chance to try the 2v1 mode yet.
Final Fantasy Explorers
A more-accessible Monster Hunter, geared towards Final Fantasy fans. I liked that combat moved a lot faster and the way that you level up your skills has a ton of potential, but the rest of the game is a watered-down Monster Hunter.
Hyrule Warriors
Dynasty Warriors, re-skinned with Legend of Zelda characters. Super heavy on the fan service, but ok if you'd like to play a game where you kill thousands of enemies in a match. Has two-player co-op that makes things a little easier, although I didn't struggle much in normal mode singleplayer.
Splatoon
I was really impressed by this one. Nintendo took the things that make shooters fun and then stripped away all of the parts that made them too adult or less fun, leaving behind a solid core with an interesting movement mechanic. Matches are super fast, which makes it great to drop in and play a few rounds of anytime.
Story of Seasons
This is the latest Harvest Moon game (the trademark is apparently still owned by Natsume). They've streamlined the farming/crop growing, but there's a really punishing stamina system that ended up pushing me towards just min-maxing my daily routine in a way I'm not sure I liked.
Etrian Mystery Dungeon
A party-based roguelike; I really wanted to like this, but I'm not super into roguelikes. When your party dies, it's a significant setback.
Star Realms
A nice and simple deck-building game that's quick to pick up and play but still has room for good strategizing. Has expansions but they're all set additions of ~15 cards vs. a TCG's booster pack style.
7 Wonders: Duel
Kind of really complicated. Has a neat drafting mechanic for you to build your civilization from though.
Star Fox Zero
When I learned this was coming I got really excited; I remember playing a ton of Star Fox 64. But they went full Wii U, and the game feels more like a tech demo - the controls made it too hard for me to actually get past the third level, and I dropped it. Someone from Polygon couldn't make it through either and sums up my thoughts better.
Pokken Tournament
I guess this is Tekken? But with Pokemon? I haven't played Tekken in a very long time. It's a decent enough fighting game, as someone who doesn't really play fighting games. No complaints (although everyone wiped the floor with me in online mode).
Steamworld Dig
I first played this on the PC a few years back, but got a chance to come back to it for 3DS this year. It's still just as good; super satisfying core game loop (dig for money, use money for upgrades so you can dig deeper for more money for more upgrades).
Freedom Planet
Kind of a cluttered, harder to understand Sonic the Hedgehog.
Shantae and the Pirate's Curse
This reminds me a lot of the games that I used to play on the Sega Genesis for some reason. I wasn't really able to get into it.
Affordable Space Adventures
A cute little side scroller puzzler game. Really interesting control scheme where your Wii U gamepad controls the different ship systems.
Pocket Card Jockey
Adorably horse racing solitaire that doesn't make any sense but is oddly super compelling. I can't stop playing either.

Things Read (32)

Altered Carbon: Richard K. Morgan; SF
In the future your brain can be downloaded into new bodies; a man who committed suicide is sure he was murdered.
Broken Angels: Richard K. Morgan; SF
In the same brain downloading future, a man tries to avoid a war by digging up a spaceship.
Storm Front: Jim Butcher, Fantasy
The first Harry Dresden book; recommended by Kitt. Does a good job world building.
The Cold Dish: Craig Johnson, Mystery
The first Walt Longmire book; also recommended by Kitt. Some parts were hard to follow because I don't know American native history, but fun to read. Very twisty.
War Dogs: Greg Bear, SF
Space marines on mars. There's a lot of winding around before the plot gets to the point.
The Circle: Dave Eggers
An unnerving take on what working at a Big Internet Company™ might be like in the future.
The Shell Collector: Hugh Howey
A neat globe-trotting story about high-value shell collecting.
The Hard Thing About Hard Things: Ben Horowitz, Business
Ben Horowitz tells a lot of stories about how he repeatedly pulled the fat out of the fire, and how you will need to do that too some day. Has a good section about one on ones.
Woken Furies: Richard K. Morgan; SF
A story develops from a grudge and a bar fight, and progresses from there. Lots of sci fi action.
The Icarus Deception: Seth Godin; Business
A focused rallying cry to Make More Art.
Hollow World: Michael J Sullivan; Thriller
A man inadverently travels farther into the future than he means to, ending up as the last man on earth.
Good to Great: Jim Collins; Business
The published results of a study on how companies go from being good, bad, or mediocre to great - lots of fascinating stories about each of the companies studied (and the ones they were compared to that didn't do as well).
Payments Systems in the U.S.
A pretty neat deep dive into all the different ways that money moves around in the United States.
Fall of the Core: Netcast 01
A short novella that's told almost entirely from the perspective of a newscaster covering the end of the world. Not as good as I was hoping.
The Better Man Project
Basically a condensed collection of the tips from Men's Health, grouped into chapters by focus. Some useful things, but mostly - eat more produce and exercise more.
The End of All Things: John Scalzi; Military SF
I really enjoyed these four interwoven novellas; I haven't read Old Man's War yet, but after reading these I am definitely going to have to check it out.
Old Man's War: John Scalzi; Military SF
The Ghost Brigades: John Scalzi; Military SF
The Last Colony: John Scalzi; Military SF
The Human Division: John Scalzi; Military SF
Serial Winner: 5 Actions to Create Your Cycle of Success: Larry Weidel, Business
A kind-of interesting take on what you should be doing in your life in order to repeatedly be a winner.
Dark Intelligence: Neal Asher, SF
This is set in Neal Asher's Polity universe, but a lot more focused on the AIs than previous novels have been (or at least, that I've read). Moves pretty fast, I really enjoyed it.
The Lazarus War: Artefact: Jamie Sawyer, Military SF
I read this after stumbling accross a recommendation for it on Neal Asher's blog; the story didn't really go where I expected it to, but it was a decent enough read for me to try the second book.
The Lazarus War: Legion: Jamie Sawyer, Military SF
This was closer to what I was expecting out of the first book. Neat little twists.
Leviathan Wakes (The Expanse Book 1): James S.A. Corey, SF
A grey goo story, but with an interesting enough set of characters and world design that I was up for reading the rest of the series. Grey goo is only needed for the initial setup; I enjoyed all of the books.
Caliban's War (The Expanse Book 2): James S.A. Corey, SF
Abaddon's Gate (The Expanse Book 3): James S.A. Corey, SF
Cibola Burn (The Expanse Book 4): James S.A. Corey, SF
Nemesis Games (The Expanse Book 5): James S.A. Corey, SF
Big Magic: Creative Living Beyond Fear: Elizabeth Gilbert
A pretty nice collection of anecdotes about living a creative life and having the courage to continue to create, whether you pass or fail. I found this very motivating.
De Profundis: Oscar Wilde
Oscar Wilde wrote a letter, and some parts of it will be relevant to your life right now and some parts might only be relevant a year from now. I feel like you could read this once a year and get something new out each time.
Lamb: Christopher Moore, Humor
What if Jesus best friend had written an account of all the trouble they got up to? A good palate cleanser after De Profundis.
